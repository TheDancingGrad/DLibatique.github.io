1. Identify: Gerunds, Gerundives, and Supines

Identify the construction contained in the following sentences: gerund, gerundive, accusative supine, or ablative supine; then, translate the sentence.

  1. ibam venditum boves maturos familiae. <vendo, -ere, vendidi, venditus = “to sell”>


  2. Cicero in rostra escendit ad orationem incipiendam. <escendo, -ere, escendi, escensus = “to climb up, mount” (with in + acc.)>


  3. Cicero in rostra escendit ad dicendum.


  4. cogitavimus eum mortuum esse, sed -- mirabile dictu! -- adhuc vivebat. <adhuc = “still”>


  5. iuste amiceque regendo rex carus populo factus est.


  6. si locutum bona ad me veniretis, vos laete acciperem. <laetus, -a, -um = “happy”>


  7. a nobilibus non cognitum est regnum tam difficile rectu futurum esse.


  8. domini servis placendi sunt.


  9. longissimae vitae vivendae pater meus cupidus est.


  10. scivistine orationes meas ab omnibus in foro auditum iri?


responsa ostende!
  1. ibam venditum boves maturos familiae.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Accusative Supine
    Correct translation: I went in order to sell my family's mature bulls.

  2. Cicero in rostra escendit ad orationem incipiendam.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Gerundive
    Correct translation: Cicero mounts / mounted the rostrum to begin his speech.

  3. Cicero in rostra escendit ad dicendum.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Gerund
    Correct translation: Cicero mounts / mounted the rostrum to speak.

  4. cogitavimus eum mortuum esse, sed -- mirabile dictu! -- adhuc vivebat.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Ablative Supine
    Correct translation: We thought that he had died, but -- miraculous to say! -- he was still alive.

  5. iuste amiceque regendo rex carus populo factus est.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Gerund
    Correct translation: By ruling justly and in a friendly manner, the king became dear to his people.

  6. si locutum bona ad me veniretis, vos laete acciperem.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Accusative Supine
    Correct translation: If you were coming to me to say good things, I would receive you happily.

  7. a nobilibus non cognitum est regnum tam difficile rectu futurum esse.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Ablative Supine
    Correct translation: It was not recognized by the nobles that the kingdom would be so difficult to rule.

  8. domini servis placendi sunt.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Gerundive
    Correct translations: Masters must be pleased by the slaves; slaves must please the masters.

  9. longissimae vitae vivendae pater meus cupidus est.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Gerundive
    Correct translation: My father is desirous of living the longest life.

  10. scivistine orationes meas ab omnibus in foro auditum iri?
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Accusative Supine
    Correct translation: Did you know that my speeches would be heard by everyone in the forum?

2. Change: Gerunds and Gerundives

Change the bolded gerund phrases into their gerundive equivalents. Give the changed phrase as your answer.

  1. venit ad multa dicendum.

  2. loquendo pauca hostes diem dixerunt.

  3. ad litteras legendum eis luce opus est.

  4. omnes leges didicit ad cives bene regendum.

  5. patres conscripti Catonem cupidissimum Carthaginem delendi esse sciverunt. <{illi} patres conscripti, patrum conscriptorum = “conscript fathers, Senators”>
responsa ostende!
  1. venit ad multa dicendum.
    Your answer:
    Correct answer: ad multa dicenda

  2. loquendo pauca hostes diem dixerunt.
    Your answer:
    Correct answer: loquendis paucis

  3. ad litteras legendum eis luce opus est.
    Your answer:
    Correct answer: ad litteras legendas

  4. omnes leges didicit ad cives bene regendum.
    Your answer:
    Correct answer: ad cives bene regendos

  5. patres conscripti Catonem cupidissimum Carthaginem delendi esse sciverunt.
    Your answer:
    Correct answer: Carthaginis delendae

3. Identify: Subordinate Subjunctive Clauses

Identify whether the sentence contains a clause of prevention, a clause of proviso, or a temporal clause. Then translate.

  1. gaudebam dummodo gauderes.


  2. magnum periculum me non deterruit quominus ex urbe proficiscerer.


  3. stilum sustulit priusquam cognosceret quid scriberet.


  4. amicum retinui quominus in viam curreret.


  5. dum aliquis eam bene doceat discere philosophiam potero.

responsa ostende!
  1. gaudebam dummodo gauderes.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Proviso
    Correct translation: I was happy provided that you were happy.

  2. magnum periculum me non deterruit quominus ex urbe proficiscerer.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Prevention
    Correct translation: The great danger did not prevent me from setting out from the city.

  3. stilum sustulit priusquam cognosceret quid scriberet.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Temporal
    Correct translation: He picked up his pen before he could understand what he was to write.

  4. amicum retinui quominus in viam curreret.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Prevention
    Correct translation: I held my friend back from running into the road.

  5. dum aliquis eam bene doceat discere philosophiam potero.
    Your answer:
    Your translation:
    Correct answer: Proviso
    Correct translation: Provided that someone teaches it well, I will be able to learn philosophy.

4. Translate

Translate the following sentences into idiomatic English.

  1. semper mihi metus est ne domi nescio quid improvisum inveniam. <improvisus, -a, -um = “unforeseen”>

  2. Orpheus cogitavit Eurydicem, quae mortua esset, e tenebris redituram esse, sed ille erravit. <{ille} Orpheus, -i = “Orpheus”; {illa} Eurydice, -es = “Eurydice”>

  3. mihi in cubiculo manere imperatum est donec hospites abiisse audirem. <{ille} hospes, hospitis = “guest”>

  4. in cubiculo manebam donec hospites abiisse audivi.

  5. postquam e foro profecti erant ad portum iere amicos exspectatum. <{ille} portus, -us = “port”; sto, stare, steti, status = “to stand”; exspecto, -are, -avi, -atus = “to wait for, await”>

  6. ne prius discesseritis quam magister cuique vestrum dicat.

  7. duos annos Athenis vixit, sed et Carthagini domum habet.

  8. si oculi vere portae mentis essent, perpetuo cognoscere quid cogitares possem. <{illa} porta, -ae = “gate”>

  9. non te prohibui quominus parentes vocares quia te melius quam ego iuvarent.

  10. forsitan redeamus si nobis locum in quo maneamus dare velint.
responsa ostende!
  1. semper mihi metus est ne domi nescio quid improvisum inveniam.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: I always have the fear that I will find something unforeseen at home.

  2. Orpheus cogitavit Eurydicem, quae mortua esset, e tenebris redituram esse, sed ille erravit.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: Orpheus thought that Eurydice, who had died, would return from the shadows, but he was mistaken.

  3. mihi in cubiculo manere imperatum est donec hospites abiisse audirem.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: It was ordered to me to stay in my room until I should hear that the guests had left.

  4. in cubiculo manebam donec hospites abiisse audivi.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: I stayed in my room until I heard that the guests had left.

  5. postquam e foro profecti erant ad portum iere amicos exspectatum.
    Your answer:
    Correct answer: After they had set out from the forum, they went to the port to wait for their friends.

  6. ne prius discesseritis quam magister cuique vestrum dicat.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: Do not leave before your teacher speaks to each of you.

  7. duos annos Athenis vixit, sed et Carthagini domum habet.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: He has lived in Athens for two years, but he also has a home in Carthage.

  8. si oculi vere portae mentis essent, perpetuo cognoscere quid cogitares possem.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: If the eyes were truly the gates of the mind, I would always be able to understand what you were thinking.

  9. non te prohibui quominus parentes vocares quia te melius quam ego iuvarent.
    Your translation:
    Correct translation: I did not prohibit you from calling your parents because they would help you better than I.

  10. forsitan redeamus si nobis locum in quo maneamus dare velint.
    Your answer:
    Correct answer: Perhaps we would return if they should want to give us a place in which to stay.
